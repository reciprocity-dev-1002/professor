The Five Concepts You'll Choose From

Vertical Channel Conflict — disagreement between different levels of the channel (e.g., manufacturer vs. retailer)
Horizontal Channel Conflict — disagreement between members at the same channel level
Coercive Channel Power — one member forces compliance by threatening penalties or withholding access
Contractual Control — using formal franchise agreements or exclusive contracts to bind and coordinate channel partners
Direct Channel Disruption — a company bypasses traditional intermediaries to control the path to the customer

Debrief: The Framework in Four Companies

Apple — Coercive Channel Power

Apple doesn't just participate in a channel — it built the channel and then locked the door. The App Store is coercive because developers have no alternative route to iPhone users. The 30% fee, the review process, and the no-sideloading rule all flow from that single chokehold. The question isn't whether Apple provides value — it does. The question is whether any company should have that kind of unilateral power over a $2 trillion ecosystem.

Ticketmaster — Coercive Channel Power + Vertical Conflict

Ticketmaster controls access to the venue, which is the only legal channel to the fan. The venue signs an exclusive contract; the fan has no choice. When Ticketmaster also owns the promoter (Live Nation), vertical conflict is built into the business model — the same company is competing with itself at every level. The Taylor Swift collapse wasn't a technology failure. It was a channel power failure.

Subway — Vertical Channel Conflict + Contractual Control

The $5 Footlong was a marketing win at the corporate level and a financial loss at the franchisee level. Corporate sets the price; franchisees pay the cost. That's textbook vertical channel conflict — two different levels of the channel with opposite incentives. Franchise contracts give Subway the legal right to enforce pricing. But legal authority and smart channel management are not the same thing.

John Deere — Vertical Channel Conflict + Coercive Power

John Deere extended its channel control past the point of sale and into the customer's barn — years after the transaction. The software lock isn't a safety feature. It's a mechanism for routing service revenue through authorized dealers indefinitely. Farmers who are locked out of their own equipment during harvest season have zero negotiating power. That's coercive channel power operating through product design rather than a contract.
